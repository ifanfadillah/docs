This page provides an explanation of definitions for Rate Card (Price Estimation) and CPM for an advertising screen:

## Rate Card (Price Estimation)

The Rate Card is essentially the "sticker price" or the baseline gross cost to rent a specific billboard for a standard duration (typically per month, per week, or per day).

This is the absolute budget you need to secure the inventory. However, a high Rate Card does not necessarily mean the screen is "expensive" in terms of value, and a low Rate Card does not mean it is "cheap." The true value is determined by how many people actually see it, which is where CPM comes into play.

---

## CPM (Cost Per Mille / eCPM)

"Mille" is Latin for one thousand. CPM (or effective CPM) is the great equalizer in advertising. It represents the exact financial cost required for your advertisement to be seen 1,000 times (1,000 impressions) by the audience.

Media buyers use CPM to compare the cost-efficiency of different screens apples-to-apples. For example, Billboard A might have a Rate Card of \$10,000/month, and Billboard B might cost \$2,000/month. If Billboard A is on a massive highway and generates millions of impressions, its CPM might be significantly lower than the smaller Billboard B. Therefore, Billboard A is actually the more cost-efficient choice for mass reach.

### The CPM Formula

To calculate the Cost Per Mille, you need the Total Cost (the Rate Card price) and the Total Estimated Impressions for that same time period\[cite: 312\].

The standard formula is:

$CPM = \left( \frac{\text{Total Cost}}{\text{Total Impressions}} \right) \times 1000$

**Example Calculation:**

Let's say you are renting a digital billboard for one month\[cite: 314\]:

- **Rate Card (Total Cost):** \$5,000 
- **Total Monthly Impressions:** 2,500,000 

$CPM = \left( \frac{5,000}{2,500,000} \right) \times 1000$

$CPM = 0.002 \times 1000$

**CPM = \$2.00**

_This means you are paying exactly \$2.00 for every 1,000 times your ad is exposed to the audience._
